How this application will syc with existing e-commerce websites?

Synchronization between this application and an existing e-commerce website (like Shopify, Magento, or WooCommerce) happens automatically through a combination of Webhooks, Event-Driven API Calls, and Background Jobs.

Because we have built a highly decoupled architecture using FastAPI and Celery workers, the sync works in real-time without slowing down either system. Here is the technical breakdown of how data flows between the two:

1. Inbound Sync (Website -> Our Application)
When a customer places an order on the external e-commerce website, we need it to show up in our Dashboard immediately.

How it works: You configure a Webhook on the e-commerce site (e.g., Shopify) that triggers every time an "Order is Created."
The Route: The webhook sends a JSON payload instantly to our application’s inbound webhook URL (/api/webhooks/orders).
Processing: Our FastAPI backend securely receives this JSON, extracts the customer and product details, and maps it directly into our Orders table as a pending fulfillment order.
2. Outbound Sync (Our Application -> Website)
If a retail cashier sells a shirt using our POS in the physical store, we need to tell the e-commerce website to lower its inventory so it doesn't accidentally oversell.

How it works (Event-Driven): The moment an order is confirmed in our POS or dashboard, the system triggers an event.
n8n / Middleware: We natively support sending outbound webhook events (via n8n or direct POST requests) back to the e-commerce platform's API (e.g., hitting the Shopify Inventory API).
Result: The e-commerce website’s stock is reduced by 1 in real-time.
3. Background Reconciliation (The Safety Net)
Webhooks can sometimes drop if there's a temporary internet outage. To prevent data drift, we use our built-in Celery Background Workers.

How it works: We run scheduled tasks (e.g., every hour or midnight).
The Process: Our Celery worker wakes up, reaches out to the e-commerce site's REST API, and says, "Give me all orders and inventory changes from the last hour." It compares that data against our database and patches any discrepancies.
In Short
Orders: Flow seamlessly from the Website to Our Dashboard via Inbound Webhooks.
Inventory: Flows outwards from Our Dashboard to the Website via Event-driven API calls when items are sold or received.
Stability: Handled entirely in the background by asynchronous Celery workers so the merchant's UI never freezes while waiting for syncs.

An existing e-comm website may have their own db and this application has different db. How product/inventory will be managed and controlled?

This is the exact problem our platform was built to solve. When you have two separate databases (your E-commerce DB and our Operations DB), you need a strategy to keep them perfectly in sync without corrupting data.

Here is exactly how our application manages and controls inventory across both databases:

1. The SKU is the Universal Bridge
To connect the two databases, we rely on the SKU (Stock Keeping Unit) as the universal identifier. For example, if a black shirt in size Large is SHIRT-BLK-L in our system, it MUST be SHIRT-BLK-L on the e-commerce website.

When a sync happens, our system says: "Update the inventory for SHIRT-BLK-L." The e-commerce site knows exactly which product to update, regardless of its internal database structure.

2. We Act as the "Master" (Source of Truth)
In a dual-database setup, you must define which system is the "Master." Our platform acts as the OMS (Order Management System) and PIM (Product Information Manager):

Product Creation: You create the product (and its size/color variants) in our application. We then automatically push that product definition to the e-commerce site's database.
Inventory Mastery: We track the physical inventory in actual warehouses and bins. The e-commerce site only receives a "Total Available" number from us.
3. The SKUPlatformMapping Engine
Under the hood, we have a specialized database table called SKUPlatformMapping.

When we push SHIRT-BLK-L to Shopify, Shopify creates it in their database and assigns it an internal ID (e.g., 123456789).
Shopify sends that ID back to us.
We store it in our SKUPlatformMapping table: [Our SKU: SHIRT-BLK-L] -> [Shopify ID: 123456789].
Next time our warehouse receives 50 more of those shirts, we use that exact Shopify ID to update their database directly, preventing any mismatches.
4. "Buffer" / Safety Stock Allocation
To prevent overselling (when an item sells online and in-store at the exact same millisecond), our system allows for Allocation Buffers.

If you have 10 physical units of a shirt in the warehouse, our system might only push "8 units available" to the e-commerce database.
The 2 remaining units are kept as a "safety buffer" for the physical store.
Summary: Your e-commerce website acts as a "dumb storefront." It just displays what our application tells it to display, and it passes orders back to our application for actual fulfillment and accounting.



Does Indian market needs B2B with this application?
Absolutely. In fact, for the Indian garment industry, the B2B capabilities might be your biggest selling point.

While B2C (selling to the end consumer) gets all the glamour, the true volume in the Indian textile and garment market flows through deeply entrenched B2B distribution networks.

Here is why a B2B Wholesale Portal is a massive necessity for the Indian market specifically:

1. The "WhatsApp & Catalog" Problem
Right now, garment manufacturers in hubs like Surat, Tirupur, and Ludhiana sell to thousands of small boutiques and retailers across India.

How they do it currently: They spam WhatsApp groups with PDFs of "catalogs." The retailer replies saying, "Send me 2 sets of Design A and 3 sets of Design B." The manufacturer manually checks stock, makes a handwritten bill or uses Tally, and chases for payment. It is a logistical nightmare.
How your app solves it: You give the manufacturer a private B2B portal. The retailer logs in, sees exactly what is in stock (in the exact Size × Color matrix), places the bulk order, and it flows directly into your Dashboard.
2. Credit Management (Udhari)
The Indian B2B garment trade runs on credit (Net-30, Net-60 terms). E-commerce platforms like Shopify require payment upfront via credit card, which is why Indian wholesalers hate using them. Our application's Draft/Quotation workflow and payment_status = pending tracking are perfectly suited for managing ledgers and tracking who owes money.

3. Strict GST Compliance (Input Tax Credit)
In India, B2B sales require a very specific type of invoice so the buyer can claim Input Tax Credit (ITC).

Shopify struggles with complex Indian GST (IGST vs CGST/SGST) splits and capturing the buyer's GSTIN dynamically.
Our backend (order.py) is already engineered with fields for customer_gstin, hsn_code, igst_amount, etc., making it perfectly positioned to generate compliant B2B invoices out of the box.
